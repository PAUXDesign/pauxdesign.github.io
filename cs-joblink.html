Overview

JobLink lets users manage multiple career profiles and apply across industries without switching accounts. The key insight: 43% of job seekers apply across 2+ industries but every platform assumes one career identity.

I ran the full process — user research, competitive analysis, card sorting, wireframing, A/B testing and high-fidelity iOS UI.

The problem
"Users were spending 15+ hours a week just customising applications. Many gave up on roles they were qualified for — it was simply too much work."
User pain points
  • Creating multiple accounts (against platform policies)
  • Rewriting profiles for every industry
  • One-size-fits-all CV that stands out nowhere
  • 15+ hours/week lost to customisation
  • Abandoning qualified roles — too much friction
Research findings
  • 59% wanted multiple career profiles
  • 70% passively job hunting — time is scarce
  • 98% search for jobs near their location
  • Users want quick-scan cards, not descriptions upfront
  • Profile switching mental model already known (Instagram)

Design process
Phase 01
User research & surveys

Key insight: the biggest pain wasn't finding jobs — it was the time cost of tailoring each application.

Phase 02
Competitive analysis

Analysed Indeed and TotalJobs. Gaps: no multi-profile support, poor tracking, no distance search — these became JobLink's differentiators.

Phase 03
Card sorting & IA

Organised into four sections: Home, Applications, Messages, Profile. Flowcharts and wireframes to validate navigation before visuals.

Phase 04
A/B testing

My assumption — detailed descriptions upfront — was wrong. Users wanted quick-scan cards to filter fast. This changed the entire IA.

Impact on behaviour
Before JobLink
  • 3 resumes in Google Drive, manually managed
  • 20+ minutes per application, customising everything
  • Often abandoned mid-application — decision fatigue
After JobLink
  • 2 profiles created once, one-tap apply
  • 5 jobs applied in the time it used to take for 1
  • Users reported feeling "in control" of their search
Key learnings
🔍
Design for the exception, not the average: Identify the underserved edge case and design for them first — career changers, multi-skilled professionals.
🧪
A/B testing proved my assumptions wrong: Always test before committing to an IA decision.
✂️
Reducing cognitive load beats adding features: Profile switching and one-tap apply were loved. Smart suggestions were ignored. Simplicity is the feature.
